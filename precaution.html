Earthquake Safety Protocols

Before an Earthquake

  • Secure heavy furniture, appliances, and hanging items to prevent them from falling.
  • Create an emergency plan and practice earthquake drills with your family.
  • Prepare an emergency kit with food, water, medications, first aid supplies, and important documents.
  • Know how to turn off gas, water, and electricity in your home.
  • Identify safe spots in each room, such as under sturdy tables or against interior walls.

During an Earthquake

  • Drop, Cover, and Hold On - Drop to the ground, take cover under a sturdy table or desk, and hold on until the shaking stops.
  • If there's no table nearby, cover your face and head with your arms and crouch in an inside corner of the building.
  • Stay away from glass, windows, outside doors and walls, and anything that could fall.
  • If you're outdoors, stay in the open away from buildings, streetlights, and utility wires.
  • If you're in a vehicle, pull over to a clear location and stop. Avoid bridges, overpasses, and power lines.

After an Earthquake

  • Check yourself and others for injuries. Provide first aid if needed.
  • Be prepared for aftershocks, which can occur minutes, hours, or days after the main quake.
  • Check for gas leaks, electrical system damage, and water line damage. If you smell gas, open windows and leave the building.
  • Stay away from damaged areas unless your assistance has been specifically requested.
  • Listen to a battery-operated radio or television for the latest emergency information.

Flood Safety Protocols

Before a Flood

  • Know your area's flood risk and elevation level.
  • Prepare an emergency kit and create a family communication plan.
  • Consider buying flood insurance if you live in a flood-prone area.
  • Elevate the furnace, water heater, and electric panel if susceptible to flooding.
  • Install check valves in plumbing to prevent floodwater from backing up into drains.

During a Flood

  • Listen to the radio or television for information and updates.
  • Be aware of streams, drainage channels, canyons, and other areas known to flood suddenly.
  • If there is any possibility of a flash flood, move immediately to higher ground.
  • Do not walk through moving water. Six inches of moving water can make you fall.
  • Do not drive into flooded areas. Turn around and go another way.

After a Flood

  • Return home only when authorities indicate it is safe.
  • Avoid standing water as it may be electrically charged from underground or downed power lines.
  • Be aware of areas where floodwaters have receded. Roads may have weakened and could collapse.
  • Clean and disinfect everything that got wet. Mud left from floodwater can contain sewage and chemicals.
  • Throw away food that has come into contact with floodwater.

Hurricane Safety Protocols

Hurricanes are powerful tropical storms with sustained winds of 74 mph or higher. They can cause catastrophic damage to coastlines and several hundred miles inland.

Key Safety Measures

  • Create an evacuation plan and follow official evacuation orders.
  • Secure your home by boarding up windows and reinforcing doors.
  • Prepare an emergency kit with at least 3 days of supplies.
  • Stay indoors during the hurricane and away from windows and glass doors.
  • Do not walk, swim, or drive through flood waters.

Tornado Safety Protocols

Before a Tornado

  • Know the signs of a tornado: dark, often greenish sky; large hail; large, dark, low-lying cloud; loud roar, similar to a freight train.
  • Have a disaster plan and an emergency kit ready.
  • Identify a safe room in your home where family members could gather during a tornado.
  • Sign up for your community's warning system. The Emergency Alert System (EAS) and NOAA Weather Radio also provide emergency alerts.
  • Practice periodic tornado drills so everyone knows what to do if a tornado approaches.

During a Tornado

  • If you're in a building, go to a pre-designated shelter area such as a safe room, basement, storm cellar, or the lowest building level.
  • If there is no basement, go to the center of an interior room on the lowest level away from corners, windows, doors, and outside walls.
  • If you're in a vehicle, trailer, or mobile home, get out immediately and go to the lowest floor of a sturdy, nearby building or storm shelter.
  • If you're outside with no shelter, lie flat in a nearby ditch or depression and cover your head with your hands. Do not get under an overpass or bridge.
  • Watch out for flying debris. Flying debris from tornadoes causes most fatalities and injuries.

After a Tornado

  • Keep listening to EAS, NOAA Weather Radio, and local authorities for updated information.
  • If you are trapped, cover your mouth with a cloth to avoid breathing dust. Try to send a text, bang on a pipe or wall, or use a whistle instead of shouting.
  • Stay clear of fallen power lines or broken utility lines.
  • Do not enter damaged buildings until you are told that they are safe.
  • Save your phone calls for emergencies. Phone systems are often down or busy after a disaster. Use text messages or social media to communicate with family and friends.

Wildfire Safety Protocols

Before a Wildfire

  • Create a defensible space around your home by clearing away flammable materials.
  • Use fire-resistant or non-combustible materials on the roof and exterior of the dwelling.
  • Keep emergency supplies, including a first aid kit, in a safe place.
  • Develop an emergency evacuation plan and make sure everyone in your family knows it.
  • Sign up for your community's warning system and know your evacuation routes.

During a Wildfire

  • Listen to emergency officials and evacuate immediately if ordered to do so.
  • If trapped, call 911 and give your location.
  • If you have time before evacuating, close all windows, vents, and doors to prevent drafts.
  • Remove lightweight curtains and move flammable furniture to the center of the room.
  • Turn off gas at the meter and turn off pilot lights.

After a Wildfire

  • Return home only when authorities say it is safe.
  • Use caution when entering burned areas as hazards may still exist.
  • Check the roof immediately and extinguish any sparks or embers.
  • Check for hot spots in your yard and extinguish them with water.
  • Wear a dust mask and wet debris down to minimize breathing dust particles.

Tsunami Safety Protocols

Before a Tsunami

  • Know if your home, school, workplace, or other frequently visited locations are in tsunami hazard areas.
  • Learn the warning signs of a tsunami: a strong earthquake, a sudden rise or fall of the ocean, or a loud roar from the ocean.
  • Make an emergency plan and prepare a disaster supply kit.
  • Know your community's evacuation routes and designated safe zones.
  • If you live in a coastal area, elevate your home to minimize flood damage.

During a Tsunami Warning

  • If you feel a strong earthquake while at the coast, move immediately to higher ground. Don't wait for official warnings.
  • If you see the water recede unusually far exposing the sea floor, run to high ground immediately.
  • Listen to emergency information and alerts.
  • Evacuate if advised by authorities. Move to higher ground or inland.
  • If you cannot get to higher ground, go to the highest floor of a sturdy building.

After a Tsunami

  • Stay away from tsunami-affected areas until officials say it is safe to return.
  • Avoid disaster areas and debris in the water; water may be contaminated.
  • Stay away from damaged buildings, roads, and bridges.
  • Check yourself for injuries and get first aid if necessary.
  • Text instead of call. Phone lines may be down or busy after a disaster.

Drought Safety Protocols

Water Conservation Measures

  • Fix leaky faucets, toilets, and pipes in your home.
  • Install water-saving devices on faucets, toilets, and appliances.
  • Take shorter showers and avoid baths, which use more water.
  • Only run dishwashers and washing machines with full loads.
  • Collect and reuse water when possible (e.g., use shower water for plants).

Outdoor Water Conservation

  • Water lawns and gardens during the coolest parts of the day to minimize evaporation.
  • Use drought-resistant plants in your landscaping.
  • Use mulch around plants to retain moisture.
  • Collect rainwater in barrels for garden use.
  • Avoid washing cars or use commercial car washes that recycle water.

Health Precautions During Drought

  • Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water, even if water restrictions are in place.
  • Be aware of air quality, as drought conditions can increase dust and allergens.
  • Watch for signs of heat-related illness, which can be more common during droughts.
  • Store emergency water supplies in case of severe water shortages.
  • Follow all local water use restrictions and conservation guidelines.

Avalanche Safety Protocols

Before Entering Avalanche Terrain

  • Check avalanche forecasts and weather conditions before heading out.
  • Take an avalanche safety course to learn about hazard assessment.
  • Always carry proper avalanche safety equipment: transceiver, probe, and shovel.
  • Never travel alone in avalanche terrain.
  • Plan your route to avoid high-risk avalanche areas.

During Avalanche Risk

  • Cross dangerous areas one person at a time to minimize risk.
  • Watch for warning signs: recent avalanche activity, cracking or collapsing snow, or hollow sounds underfoot.
  • Maintain distance between group members when crossing slopes.
  • Keep an eye on changing weather conditions.
  • Be prepared to change plans if conditions worsen.

If Caught in an Avalanche

  • Try to move to the side of the avalanche or grab onto trees or rocks.
  • Discard heavy equipment like skis or snowboards.
  • Fight to stay on top of the snow using a swimming motion.
  • Create an air pocket in front of your face before the snow settles.
  • If buried, conserve energy and oxygen by breathing slowly and remaining calm.

Volcano Safety Protocols

Before a Volcanic Eruption

  • Be aware if you live in an active volcano zone and learn about local warning systems.
  • Prepare an emergency supply kit and make a family emergency plan.
  • Have dust masks available for all family members.
  • Identify a shelter location away from areas at risk for volcanic hazards.
  • Keep your vehicle's gas tank full in case evacuation is necessary.

During a Volcanic Eruption

  • Follow evacuation orders issued by authorities.
  • Avoid areas downwind and river valleys downstream of the volcano.
  • If caught in a rockfall, roll into a ball to protect your head.
  • Wear long-sleeved shirts and pants to protect your skin from ash.
  • Use goggles and wear eyeglasses instead of contact lenses to protect your eyes from ash.

After a Volcanic Eruption

  • Stay indoors until authorities say it is safe to go outside.
  • Close all windows, doors, and dampers to keep volcanic ash from entering.
  • Avoid driving in heavy ash fall unless absolutely necessary.
  • Clear roofs of ash to prevent collapse, but be careful as ash makes surfaces slippery.
  • Avoid contact with ash as much as possible and wear a dust mask when cleaning up.

Cyclone Safety Protocols

Before a Cyclone

  • Know your area's cyclone risk and stay informed about weather forecasts.
  • Prepare an emergency kit with food, water, medications, and important documents.
  • Secure your home: trim trees, reinforce roofs, and install storm shutters.
  • Identify evacuation routes and safe shelters in your community.
  • Keep a list of emergency contact numbers readily available.

During a Cyclone

  • Stay indoors and away from windows, skylights, and glass doors.
  • Take shelter in the strongest part of your house, typically a small interior room.
  • If advised to evacuate, do so immediately.
  • Turn off utilities if instructed to do so by authorities.
  • Keep listening to the radio or TV for updates and instructions.

After a Cyclone

  • Return home only when authorities indicate it is safe.
  • Watch out for fallen power lines, damaged gas lines, and structural damage.
  • Avoid floodwaters, which may be contaminated or electrically charged.
  • Document property damage with photographs for insurance purposes.
  • Check on neighbors, especially the elderly or those with special needs.

Landslide Safety Protocols

Before a Landslide

  • Learn about landslide risk in your area and watch for warning signs like tilting trees or cracks in the ground.
  • Create and practice an evacuation plan with your family.
  • Consider a geologic evaluation of your property if you live in a high-risk area.
  • Install flexible pipe fittings to avoid gas or water leaks during a landslide.
  • Plant ground cover on slopes and build retaining walls to help prevent erosion.

During a Landslide

  • Listen for unusual sounds that might indicate moving debris, such as trees cracking or boulders knocking together.
  • If you are near a stream or channel, be alert for sudden changes in water flow or water clarity.
  • If you are in a landslide-prone area and it is safe to leave, evacuate immediately.
  • If escape is not possible, curl into a tight ball and protect your head.
  • Stay alert and awake during periods of intense rainfall, which can trigger landslides.

After a Landslide

  • Stay away from the slide area as there may be danger of additional slides.
  • Check for injured or trapped persons near the slide area, but do not enter the direct slide area.
  • Report broken utility lines and damaged roadways to appropriate authorities.
  • Check building foundations, chimneys, and surrounding land for damage.
  • Replant damaged ground as soon as possible to prevent further erosion.

Blizzard Safety Protocols

Before a Blizzard

  • Stock up on food, water, medications, and other essentials before the storm hits.
  • Prepare emergency heating equipment, such as a fireplace or wood stove.
  • Winterize your home by insulating walls and attics, caulking and weather-stripping doors and windows.
  • Keep your vehicle's gas tank full to prevent the fuel line from freezing.
  • Have emergency supplies in your vehicle, including blankets, food, water, and a first aid kit.

During a Blizzard

  • Stay indoors and avoid all travel unless absolutely necessary.
  • If you must go outdoors, wear several layers of warm, loose-fitting clothing.
  • Watch for signs of frostbite and hypothermia, such as loss of feeling or pale appearance in extremities.
  • Conserve fuel by keeping your residence cooler than normal and temporarily closing off heat to some rooms.
  • If using alternative heating sources, ensure proper ventilation to avoid carbon monoxide poisoning.

After a Blizzard

  • Check on neighbors, especially elderly or disabled individuals.
  • Clear snow from roofs to prevent collapse, but be careful of ice dams and falling snow.
  • Be careful when shoveling snow, as it is physically strenuous work that can lead to heart attacks.
  • Watch for downed power lines and report them immediately.
  • Be aware of potential flooding as snow begins to melt.

Heatwave Safety Protocols

Before a Heatwave

  • Install window air conditioners snugly and insulate if necessary.
  • Check air-conditioning ducts for proper insulation.
  • Install temporary window reflectors to reflect heat back outside.
  • Cover windows that receive morning or afternoon sun with drapes or shades.
  • Learn to recognize the symptoms of heat-related illnesses.

During a Heatwave

  • Stay indoors as much as possible and limit exposure to the sun.
  • Drink plenty of water, even if you don't feel thirsty. Avoid alcohol and caffeine.
  • Eat light, cool, easy-to-digest foods such as fruit or salads.
  • Wear lightweight, light-colored, loose-fitting clothing.
  • Take cool showers or baths to lower your body temperature.

Protecting Vulnerable People

  • Check on elderly neighbors, family members, and friends who may be more susceptible to heat.
  • Never leave children or pets alone in closed vehicles.
  • Ensure pets have plenty of fresh water and shade.
  • If you don't have air conditioning, go to a public place that does, such as a library or shopping mall.
  • Know the symptoms of heat exhaustion and heat stroke, and seek medical help immediately if someone shows these signs.

Storm Safety Protocols

Before a Storm

  • Secure outdoor objects that could blow away or cause damage.
  • Remove dead or rotting trees and branches that could fall during a storm.
  • Know your area's risk for different types of storms and stay informed about weather forecasts.
  • Prepare an emergency kit with essentials like food, water, flashlights, and batteries.
  • Have a family communication plan in case you are separated during the storm.

During a Storm

  • Stay indoors and away from windows during high winds or lightning.
  • If you hear thunder, go indoors immediately. Lightning can strike up to 10 miles from rainfall.
  • Avoid using electrical equipment and telephones during thunderstorms.
  • If you are driving, pull over to a safe place away from trees and power lines.
  • If flooding occurs, move to higher ground immediately and avoid walking or driving through floodwaters.

After a Storm

  • Stay away from storm-damaged areas to avoid hazards like downed power lines.
  • Report downed power lines and broken gas lines immediately to the utility company.
  • Use caution when entering buildings and watch for hidden damage.
  • Document any property damage with photographs for insurance purposes.
  • Check on neighbors, especially those who are elderly or have special needs.
